Jupiter

Appearance

Jupiter appears bright white and is a very obvious sight in the night sky. Like Venus, it can be seen in twilight, and it always shines brighter than the brightest star in the night sky - Sirius (magnitude -1.46). Depending on Jupiter's position with respect to the Earth, it can vary in visual magnitude from as bright as -2.9 at opposition down to -1.6 during conjunction with the Sun. Viewed from the Earth, Jupiter is seen to move through about one zodiac constellation (ca. 30 degrees) each year.

Brilliance

Jupiter's albedo is 52% which is the second highest of all the planets. Jupiter's atmosphere is approximately 75% hydrogen and 24% helium by mass, with the remaining one percent of the mass consisting of other elements. The outermost layer of the atmosphere contains crystals of frozen ammonia which are highly reflective.
Jupiter is the largest of all the planets and relatively close to the Sun - which makes it the fourth brightest object in the sky after the Sun, Moon, and Venus.

Observation

Jupiter has long visibility period - about 360 days in each one of the apparitions that last for 398 days. Jupiter reaches maximum brilliance during opposition. As viewed from Earth, Jupiter lies directly opposite the Sun. It rises at sunset, sets at sunrise, and is visible in the sky all night. Opposition is also the point when the planet lies closest to Earth, so Jupiter shines brightest and appears largest.
